How it works

Text and sound, analyzed together

Two branches process lyrics and audio independently, then fuse into a single representation that drives five simultaneous predictions.

Text branch
RoBERTa reads the lyrics

Full song lyrics (up to 512 tokens) are fed into frozen RoBERTa-base. The 768-dim CLS embedding captures vocabulary, syntax, and lyrical themes — then projected to 256 dims for fusion.

full lyrics roberta-base 768-dim CLS 12 frozen layers
Audio branch
Librosa reads the sound

A 30-second audio clip is analyzed for timbral texture, melody, rhythm, and brightness. A small MLP (20→128→256) compresses this into a matched-dimension audio embedding.

MFCCs ×13 chroma spectral centroid tempo ZCR
Fusion 256 + 256 → 512-dim

Both embeddings are concatenated and passed through a fusion layer, then into five output heads that predict valence, decade, energy, danceability, and tempo simultaneously using homoscedastic uncertainty weighting.

Research findings

How the model distinguishes decades

The audio branch provides the decisive signal. Without it, RoBERTa alone barely beats a random baseline — lyrics vocabulary is less decade-specific than audio texture.

Decade accuracy: multimodal vs. text-only

3.2× above random chance

The multimodal model achieves 45.3% accuracy on a 7-class decade problem where random chance is 14.3%. The 1960s (66%) and 2000s (57%) are the strongest — decades with the most distinctive linguistic and acoustic signatures.

Adjacent decades are the hardest

The 1980s (36%) and 1990s (28%) are the most confused decades — frequently misclassified as each other. This reflects genuine musical continuity: decade boundaries are arbitrary, but style evolves gradually.

Valence correlates moderately (r = 0.546)

Predicted valence correlates with Librosa-derived labels at r = 0.546, MSE = 0.0109. The model captures general emotional tone but struggles with extremes — very happy and very sad songs both get pulled toward the middle.

Null embedding prevents audio-missing collapse

A learned null embedding replaces missing audio vectors. With 20% modality dropout during training, the model gracefully degrades to lyrics-only at inference — 6% of the dataset had no audio available.

Research finding

Emotional drift across decades

Mean valence (emotional positivity) computed from Librosa audio features across 14,000 songs. Higher = more positive/happy. Lower = more negative/melancholic.

Average Valence by Decade (1960s–2020s)

2000s peaked highest

Mean valence of 0.575 — the most emotionally positive decade in the dataset, dominated by upbeat pop and early hip-hop.

1960s slightly lower

Despite the era's reputation for optimism, valence (0.538) reflects that Librosa measures acoustic brightness, not lyrical sentiment.

2010s most melancholic

Lowest mean valence at 0.554 — consistent with the rise of emotionally darker genres like emo-rap, lo-fi, and moody pop in that era.
